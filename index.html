I couldn't quite place where I was when I first met her.
It must've been summer. Not a whole lot to do before bed those days, read and listen to some music, maybe write a bit of what was on my mind. Things were quiet but in a good way, enough excitement to keep me occupied and enough downtime to really soak in my surroundings. The trees were hypnotically green, the air was cool at night and the sunsets never failed to impress, not once. I'd wake up early, earlier than I'd ever had consistently and get my day off to a good start. Needless to say the weeks flew by. A turbulent time, blurry and colorful and fuzzy around the edges.
I enjoyed much of it, and new immediately I'd grow to miss it in the future. Not fair how life does that to you.

But where was I? Hard to say, hard to put a time and a place on a moment that had been with me all my life, that I'd lived a thousand iterations of in every interminable existence. That I'd dreamt, imagined, and reconsidered so many times, twirling it between my fingers, that when it finally happened I couldn't fathom it to be real.
But it had to be. Got to be. I know because I woke up that day without that pit in my stomach, you know the one. I must've been at a friends house, or I think I went out for a run on a cool day, or I woke up on the beach and there she was. Can't say. Maybe I was on a walk after dark, or wandering the forest trails I could find, or doing something, anything, a million things of no consequence all wrapped up in my own misinterpretations of it all before she ever came around.

I had it with me all the time, carrying it in my pockets and wearing it’s stripes on my face. I’d tie my boots tight, always stepping over the debris of some runaway dream, marks of it on the underside of those warm days of spring. Though I couldn’t remember the contents of it I could remember it’s shape and the way it felt in my hands. Almost as if I could swim in it, a pool of deep green water that let me float weightless. Suspended on my back I’d look up and watch the trees sway effortlessly overhead. Something promising and regal about them, something shared and honored.

Waking up felt like a lie, bone dry and monotone. The palm fronds no longer shrouding me from the sun; the sound of the ripples no longer filling my ears. I’d sit grasping frayed ends of memory for why I dreamt what I dreamt only to lose it. Nothing immediately clear enough to be recollected yet still sharp around the edges, enough so that I’d slip and cut my fingers on it. However frustrating it might’ve been, I couldn’t help but smell the sweetness it left behind when I’d wake. I’d dress and walk, carrying with me the shreds of misremembered moments, scraps that I couldn’t glue together with enough glue or stitch together with enough thread: my sister’s thread she never used yet always seemed to be in the wrong place. I’d find it on the couch, under the table, and on my clothes, the scraps of her that I carried with me too.
